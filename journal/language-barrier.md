# Language Barrier

I'm from Iran, and I don't speak Turkish. English is the only language I can use to communicate here. That alone has turned out to be one of the harder parts of living in Turkey.

## The Reality

The overall level of English here is low. A large number of the everyday people you meet—shopkeepers, officials, service staff—don't speak any English at all, not even the basics. For simple, transactional things, you can usually get by with gestures, translation apps, or a few memorized Turkish phrases.

The real trouble starts with anything that goes beyond the basics: government offices, legal matters, or any situation where you actually need to understand what's happening and be understood in return. That's where a translation app can't save you, and where the lack of a shared language becomes a real blocker.

## How It Feels

Every time I walk into one of these situations, I feel it coming. It's disappointing—you want to get something done, something that should be straightforward, and the language barrier turns it into a struggle before you've even started.

Worse than the frustration in the moment is the uncertainty that follows. When you can't fully communicate, you can't be sure you understood things correctly, or that you were understood. You leave not knowing what's actually going to happen next. That not-knowing lingers longer than the awkward conversation itself.

This is one of the real downsides of being an expat here. It's not a single bad day—it's a recurring weight that shows up whenever something important requires more than small talk.

## My Advice

If you're thinking about moving to Turkey and you don't speak Turkish: learn the language beforehand. Even a working level of Turkish would remove a huge amount of friction from daily life here, especially for anything involving government offices, legal paperwork, or getting real help when you need it. It's far easier to build that foundation before you arrive than to try to catch up while you're already navigating everything else that comes with starting a new life in a new country.
